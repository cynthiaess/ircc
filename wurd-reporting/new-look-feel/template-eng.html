Better processes, improved outcomes

During Q4, the Web UX Research and Design (WURD) team kept refining and streamlining its processes to deliver better results for users, in a more efficient way.

The team gained a lot of maturity during this quarter, and we showed that our user-centered methods are highly successful.

Key highlights

  • Delivered multiple research projects with tens of actionable, evidence-based recommendations
  • Completed phase 1 of the Explore immigration programs tool
  • Grew the team’s research capacities

Statistics
  • In Q4 the tool had almost 250,000 visits, with 68% of visits getting to the results page at least once.

  • Our UX survey performed well, with strong quantitative results and many positive comments throughout Q4:
    • “Very excellent. Thank you for your effort”
    • “Votre assistance est appréciée!”
    • “Excellent, fast, clear, and not complicated!”

Sources: Adobe Analytics, UX survey

A new tool that:

  • addresses the challenges of the Come to Canada tool
  • offers a broad, high-level overview of different programs to help clients self-serve

What we did

  • In Q3, we launched our tool and monitored its performance on the live site
  • In Q4, we:
    • reviewed all data collected during Q3, summarized into a few additional tweaks
    • improved the Work branch by adding a few questions to provide a shorter, more tailored list of programs to clients
    • further reduced the large number of results by condensing several program streams into one result for the over-arching program
    • developed an additional branch for people seeking asylum or refugee status (pending client approval)

Next steps

  • Launch the remaining tweaks and changes planned for Phase 2
  • Informal data monitoring to ensure no usability issues arise from these tweaks. 

What we learned

  • Collecting data from multiple sources was instrumental in developing the final improvements of Phase 2

Source: Adobe Analytics

Redesign of the IRCC sign-in experience to:

  • understand the current account ecosystem
  • make it easier for clients to sign in to their existing accounts
  • prepare for the incoming Client eXperience Platform (CXP)

What we did

  • Launched the revised and improved “account chooser” page for studying in Canada, to help users identify their account, based on what they applied for
  • Re-imagined and redesigned the IRCC account wizard, to help users get back to their account, for any program with an online application. Completed a second round of usability testing, identified remaining usability issues and updated the design to fix them. Prepared for a third round of usability testing, to be completed during the Federal Budget blackout.
  • Created prototypes of all IRCC partner accounts (GCKey-using accounts) in the new sign-in pattern. Reviewed and revised text to accommodate the needs of each account.
  • Developed a prototype of the super visa content in the subway pattern. Collapsed the super visa-specific IRCC Portal landing page with the one for visitor visas. This starts the process of reducing redundancy across TR programs that point to the Portal.

Next steps

  • Complete final changes to the IRCC account wizard, seek approval and launch.
  • Monitor analytics and feedback data for the IRCC account wizard.
  • Share IRCC partner account prototypes with the Web team and launch the new design pattern.

What we learned

  • To properly direct users to their account, we need to ask them for information. We can’t present a list of all accounts – they won’t be able to correctly identify the one they need.
  • We can make existing design patterns feel more modern and lighter to use.

Preparing for the Client Experience Platform

Impact on the user journey

  1. Seek (impacted)
  2. Apply (impacted)
  3. Wait (impacted)
  4. Move forward (impacted)
  • Project to:
    • support the development of the new client platform for IRCC
    • coordinate the integration of this new platform into the existing IRCC website

What we did

  • Provided support and advice to the design team in preparation for release 2. Helped pull content from applications expected to be launched in the system.
  • Drafted changes required to the IRCC site for release 1. Liaised with web advisors to validate and approve changes.
  • Developed prototypes for the CXP launch, showing how users will be directed to the system, including integration in content and a new landing page.
  • Participated in usability testing for the account name.
  • Provided guidance and rationale on the account name to senior management in DPM.

Next steps

  • Stage and launch site updates for CXP release 1
  • Continue providing support and advice on platform and content development for release 2

What we learned

  • Clients don’t remember the name of their account. But given the existing account ecosystem, our recommendation was to use a name that stands separate from the other ones, with the end goal being no name at all.
  • More applications continue to go through the secure account than the IRCC Portal (which is where we direct clients in our content). Unless we stop new applications from being started in the legacy systems, we’ll have a hard time transitioning exclusively to the IRCC Portal – New version.

Research reports

Statistics
  • Asylum:
    • About 4 weeks to produce report and recommendations
    • 8 quick wins; 5 moderate wins; 3 big wins
  • Express Entry:
    • About 4 weeks to produce report, and recommendations, and content gap analysis
    • 12 quick wins; 4 moderate wins; 3 big wins
  • IEC:
    • About 3 weeks to produce report and recommendations
    • 14 quick wins; 3 moderate wins; 3 big wins
  • Study documents:
    • 10 days to provide rapid research review and make actionable recommendations in Figma. No formal report.
    • 8 quick wins; 2 moderate wins; 2 big wins
  • Using an Immigration or Citizenship Representative:
    • About 4 weeks to produce report and recommendations
    • 13 quick wins, 2 moderate wins.
  • We completed several in-depth research reports to support key research needs from advisors
  • We performed thorough research on:
    • Asylum
    • Express Entry
    • International Experience Canada
    • Study permit documents
    • Using an Immigration and Citizenship representative

What we did

  • Leveraged a wide variety of available data to assess user pain points
  • Conducted full-team usability review of the content to detect key usability issues
  • Made evidence- and best practice-based recommendations for improvements
  • Worked closely with advisors to validate our findings, refine our recommendations to maximize feasibility, and adapted our timelines to the urgency of the project
  • We tailored our deliverables to suit the needs of each advisor/client relationship
    • E.g. Study was under rushed timelines, so we modified our report to be shorter and more direct, delivering as soon as possible
    • IEC had a unique advisor/client relationship, so we modified our usual presentation approach to help maintain a smooth relationship between all parties

What we learned:

  • We learned that our process is very adaptable – we were able to adapt to a wide variety of deadlines and web advisor needs
  • We also experimented with different kinds of reports – our first ultra-condensed report was fully contained within Figma, saving us time by not formally creating a slide deck
  • We accepted that the relationship with program-area clients is pivotal to success. Without their trust and buy-in, even the most thorough evidence-based report could be rejected if seen as too forceful, or overbearing. We worked closely with advisors to make the handoff from WURD, to advisors, to the program client smoother than ever.
