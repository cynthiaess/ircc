Statistics

  • Over 3.2 million visits since full release of version 3 on September 3, 2024
  • 82% of users who arrived at the account wizard tool started through the tool, with 94% completing it through to an account result
  • Roughly 30% of users try to sign in on mobile
  • 83% of users move to the IRCC secure account, 8% go to the IRCC Portal, and 5% move to program content
  • About 90% want to sign in, and 10% want to create an account.

Source: Adobe Analytics

Description

  • Redesign of the IRCC sign-in experience to:
    • understand the current account ecosystem
    • make it easier for clients to sign in to their existing accounts
    • prepare for the incoming Client eXperience Platform (CXP)

What we did

  • Finalized and launched version 3 of the wizard, which made it clearer that we have multiple accounts, simplified the decision tree even more and clarified the selection to make for clients who need to create an account.
    • Monitored user activity and feedback after launch
  • Assisted in the implementation of the sign-in button on the Immigration theme page and institutional landing page.
  • Implemented the sign-in template across all partner portals, providing on-page help for known issues with GCKey and Sign-In Partner.

Next steps

  • Provide advice to Web Operations – Enterprise team about how to implement the sign-in button across more of the site and where to point it to.
  • Plan and develop version 4 of the wizard, which will integrate initial account triaging for new applicants, and linking paper applications.

What we learned

  • The majority of our clients continue to use the IRCC secure account to apply, even if it’s not the account we tell them to use in program content.
  • Clients often want to start with account creation, rather than going through program content, and were looking for the language to match.
  • Clients just want to sign in and don’t understand why we can’t simplify to that. They don’t understand the multiple account situation and how it complicates their experience.

My application

  • 1 Research
  • 2 Design
  • 3 Testing
  • 4 Launch
  • 5 Monitoring

Description

  • Project to investigate client pain points and other issues related to general application content and how to improve the My application topic page.

What we did

  • Presented findings from research to the WURD team
  • Developed multiple design options for the page and led a team discussion and design session
  • Finalized and prototyped a new design for testing
  • Prepared and launched:
    • a card sort exercise on the site, asking real users to identify where they would expect certain types of information to be
    • a first-click test for the desktop design, asking real users to identify what they would select on the page to complete pre-determined tasks

Next steps

  • Launch first-click test for mobile design and review results
  • Finalize and launch new design based on findings
  • Begin analysis of general application information in the Help Centre and develop a plan to merge that content into the main site

What we learned

  • The vast majority of general information about IRCC applications is not found within the main site, or under the My application topic.
  • Clients are looking for information about:
    • how to prepare documents and applications
    • how to update and correct applications they already submitted
    • how to find programs and the overall process
  • Accounts, application status, contacting IRCC and processing times continue to be issues that web can’t fully resolve.

Campaign pages

Description

  • Explore how campaign pages are published, and find ways to:
    • facilitate collaboration between Web, Marketing, and Strat Comms
    • improve the user experience of IRCC campaign pages
    • optimize efforts required from the Web team

What we did

  • Research activities, including:
    • Qualitative analysis of highest-traffic IRCC campaign pages
    • Many focus groups with different stakeholders (including PCO, Digital Transformation Office, Marketing ADs, and Web ADs and advisors)
  • Developed two guidance documents based on findings:
    • Intergroup alignment guidance
    • Campaign page use cases and content guidance
  • Developed new patterns and templates to improve campaign pages.
  • Published a Figma library with new patterns and templates.
  • Shared and tested the library with the coding team for feedback.

Next steps

  • Get feedback from marketing on the guidance pieces
  • Prepare a presentation of the overall guidance and new campaign library

What we learned

  • Campaign pages are often used to go beyond basic Canada.ca templates
  • Campaign pages often result in duplication of core content

WURD impact assessment

Description

  • After checking the progress of WURD’s recommendations, we decided to improve how we work with advisors with the goal of making implementing our recommendations easier for them.

What we did

  • Mapped out all recommendations from previous research reports made by WURD
  • Brainstormed questions to ask advisors on how to improve our ways of working and interacting with them

Next steps

  • Send out a survey to advisors, and conduct interviews with advisors we have worked with, as well as those we are yet to work with.
  • Create a document outlining what we have learned, and recommendations for how we should improve our methods of working with advisors and delivering our services

What we learned

  • There are a lot of factors that affect how quickly advisors can fully implement recommendations. Understanding these factors can make our work with them smoother and more pleasant
  • Getting anonymous feedback from advisors might lead to useful feedback that they might not be comfortable sharing directly with us
  • There is still room for improvement in the way we work with advisors and deliver our services
